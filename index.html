A note for President Spinelli

Babson built modern entrepreneurship education. Frontier AI is the next chapter — and Babson is already moving.

The brief that follows is short by design. The case is simple: the tools that define the next decade of building are arriving now, Babson has already taken the first steps, and the natural next move is to formalize partnerships with the three frontier labs themselves.

The Babson legacy

A track record very few institutions can claim.

For more than three decades Babson has been recognized as the global leader in entrepreneurship education — the only American business school to make entrepreneurship the center of the curriculum, not an elective on the side. The F.W. Olin Graduate School, the Babson Build Fund, the G1000 initiative, and the Summer Venture Program are not marketing language; they are infrastructure. Babson graduates start companies. Babson alumni run them at scale.

That foundation is exactly why this next move is natural. The question is not whether Babson can lead in AI-era entrepreneurship — it is whether Babson chooses to do so on its own timeline.

Already in motion

Babson + Founderz is the right beginning. The next step writes itself.

The "AI in Action" initiative with Founderz — and the AI-THINK methodology it brings to the classroom — is the right kind of first move: faculty-led, applied, framed around how entrepreneurs actually use these tools. President Spinelli has framed it well: staying competitive means understanding how to apply AI "thoughtfully and responsibly."

The natural complement is direct partnership with the labs whose models all of this rests on. Babson would not be starting from zero. Babson would be adding the upstream layer to work that is already underway.

The moment

Three labs are building the tools founders use.

Frontier AI in 2026 is, in practice, the work of three companies: Anthropic, Google, and OpenAI. Each has launched institutional education programs in the past eighteen months. Each is shipping enterprise-grade tools that go far beyond chat — Anthropic's Claude Cowork, OpenAI's Workspace Agents, Google's Workspace Intelligence — and each has spent 2026 racing past last year's capabilities.

For Babson's purposes, the labs differ in useful ways. Anthropic's Claude (Opus 4.7, released April 2026) is the strongest "architect" model — careful, literal, well-suited to high-stakes reasoning and to Anthropic's own "Learning Mode," which is designed to guide student thinking rather than just produce answers. OpenAI (GPT-5.5, also April 2026) leads on agentic execution — its "Operator" engine can navigate web tools and run multi-step workflows on a founder's behalf. Google's Gemini 3.1 Pro brings native multimodality and a one-million-token context window, with deep integration into the Workspace tools many students already use.

A partnership with all three — or with one to start — does not require choosing a winner. It puts Babson students alongside whichever lab fits the work they're doing that week.

Why Babson is built for this

The fit is unusually good.

The traits these tools reward — fast iteration, comfort with ambiguity, willingness to ship, judgment over recipe — are exactly the traits Babson has spent decades cultivating in its students. Frontier AI does not change what Babson teaches. It compresses the time between a student's idea and the first version they can put in front of a customer, sometimes from a semester to a weekend.

For a school whose graduates are measured by what they actually build, that compression is the entire game. The next page shows a handful of 2026 Summer Venture Program teams who are already living it.
