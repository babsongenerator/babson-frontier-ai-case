In practice

What a mature partnership looks like, four ways.

The four institutions below have each taken a different path to a frontier-AI partnership. The variety is the point — there is not a single template Babson would need to copy. The shared signal is that the most serious schools have moved past pilots and into institutional commitments.

Four examples

Different shapes, same direction.

The Wharton School

Universal MBA licensing · new AI major · OpenAI

Wharton has provided every full-time and executive MBA student with a ChatGPT Enterprise license since late 2024 — universal, not opt-in. Alongside the licensing, Wharton launched a dedicated MBA major in Artificial Intelligence for Business and established an Education Innovation Fund to support faculty integrating AI into instruction. The pedagogy and the tools were treated as a single rollout.

Shape
Universal seat licensing + new degree program + faculty fund.
What it signals
Wharton treats AI as core curriculum, not as an electives-track topic.

Northeastern University

Campus-wide Anthropic agreement · ~50,000 users · 13 global campuses

Northeastern signed a campus-wide agreement with Anthropic covering roughly 50,000 users across 13 global campuses. The deal foregrounds Anthropic's "Learning Mode" — designed to guide student reasoning rather than hand back answers — which is closer to how good entrepreneurship faculty already teach. Several elite international peers, including LSE and parts of Oxford, have signed similar university-wide Anthropic contracts.

Shape
Single-lab institutional partnership at large scale.
What it signals
"Learning Mode" pedagogy is the differentiator; Anthropic is the lab universities most often pick when teaching is central to the use case.

Stanford University

AIMES · $1M seed grants · faculty-driven curriculum

Stanford has invested in the curriculum layer directly. AIMES (AI Meets Education at Stanford) is a $1 million seed-grant initiative funding faculty-student projects to test new approaches to AI in teaching. The framing emphasizes deepening learning rather than short-circuiting it — Stanford funds the work that figures out what to do, then scales.

Shape
Internal grant-funded pedagogy R&D, vendor-agnostic.
What it signals
Tool access alone is not enough; the institution needs to invest in figuring out what changes in the classroom.

California State University System

~470,000 students · 63,000 faculty · ChatGPT Edu

The CSU system rolled out ChatGPT Edu to roughly 470,000 students and 63,000 faculty in 2025 — the largest institutional AI deployment in higher education. The architecture is a "walled garden": no student data or prompts feed model training. Faculty have built grading assistants and language-learning companions on the platform; the system uses scale as the lever to ensure equitable access.

Shape
System-wide deployment with data-isolation guarantees.
What it signals
When access is universal, the experimentation surface widens dramatically.
External tailwinds

The funding environment is unusually friendly.

Two outside forces are worth flagging: the Lilly Endowment's $500M AI in Higher Education initiative (with implementation grants up to $25M per institution), and the U.S. Department of Education's May 2026 priority on advancing AI in education — which will shape federal discretionary funding across several grant programs. For a Babson partnership of any tier, both create real co-funding paths.
