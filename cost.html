3 of 3 · The ask

As the #1 school for entrepreneurship, Babson is well-positioned to negotiate college-wide pricing with frontier model providers.

Babson previously ran on Google Workspace before concentrating on Microsoft 365. Re-establishing a relationship with Google would give students native access to Gemini.

For an institution like Babson, partnering with the three providers for a 5,000-user deployment involves transitioning from retail “Pro” subscriptions to negotiated Enterprise Service Agreements (ESAs). These agreements prioritize data privacy (FERPA compliance) and volume-based discounts that, in publicly-disclosed peer deals, range from roughly 40% off retail (UC Davis) to well above 80% at very large scale (CSU at $17M for nearly half a million users).

The economic landscape at 5,000 seats

Provider Partnership model Estimated annual cost Strategic leverage
OpenAI $200k – $300k Workspace Agents for end-to-end task completion; dedicated university privacy tier.
Google Gemini for Education $600k – $900k * Familiar integration model — and a return path to Google’s education stack (Workspace, Classroom, NotebookLM).
Anthropic Claude Enterprise $700k – $850k Largest context window of the three (1M tokens) — best fit for long-form research.

* Effective per-user cost is materially lower than the headline figure suggests: Google currently offers Gemini AI Pro free to verified students through June 2026, so an ESA effectively pays for faculty/staff seats while students access individually. All three figures are Babson estimates based on vendor conversations and analogous-scale peer deals, not list pricing.

How peers have negotiated

Large-scale AI deployments in 2025–2026 show that universities rarely pay sticker price. Three patterns recur:

First mover · Arizona State University

ASU was the first major institution to sign a comprehensive partnership with OpenAI. Institutions with 5,000+ users typically secure rates of $40–$60 per user per year for — compared with the $240/year a consumer pays for ChatGPT Plus.

API volume commitments · University of Michigan

U-M built “U-M GPT,” a platform that abstracts multiple foundation models (GPT, Llama, DALL·E) into one interface. By licensing via API volume commitments rather than individual seats, they avoided the rigid per-user cost structure — an approach that can be materially cheaper than per-seat pricing for organizations with predictable, concentrated usage.

Proof-of-concept credits · Industry practice

Institutions with an active innovation-lab footprint — the parallel for Babson is The Generator — can typically secure Proof-of-Concept credits: 6–12 months of free high-tier access for a subset of users, in exchange for a commitment to a full-campus rollout the following year.

A scale example

California State University’s deal with OpenAI was roughly $17 million across an initial six-month pilot and an 18-month system-wide rollout, reaching nearly half a million users — the largest publicly-disclosed peer-institution AI commitment to date. Single-school deals at Wharton, Oxford, Northeastern, and others have not been disclosed in dollars.

Our suggestion

We’d suggest supporting all three providers rather than choosing one, in roughly the order we’d phase them in:

  1. Google Gemini for Education — natural first step, given Babson’s recent history with Google Workspace and the free student tier Google currently offers.
  2. Anthropic Claude — well-suited to long-form reading, writing, and research; is built for student guidance rather than answer-delivery.
  3. OpenAI ChatGPT Edu — for students working with GPT-5.5 and on multi-step, action-oriented tasks.

Our students are already choosing among these tools, paying their own subscriptions, without institutional support. Letting them keep choosing — and backing them when they do — would meet them where they already are.
