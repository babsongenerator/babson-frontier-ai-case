Scene 1 · The premise

Three companies make the AI students use.

In 2026, three companies sit at the front of the artificial-intelligence market: OpenAI (which makes ChatGPT), Anthropic (which makes Claude), and Google (which makes Gemini). Their products are now the working tools of students at peer institutions. The next seven short scenes walk through what each does, who's using them, what they cost, and what Babson is already doing.

Model. Product. Surface.

When people say "AI," they usually mean one of two things. A is the underlying AI itself — what each of the three companies trains. A product is what you actually click on. Microsoft Copilot is a product that uses several foundation models underneath. Knowing the layers matters because the strategic relationships are at the model layer; the surfaces above shift constantly.

Three companies, three distinct strengths.

Each provider's foundation model is built around a different center of gravity. OpenAI is best when a student needs the AI to do something — click buttons, fill forms, run sequences. Anthropic is best when getting it right matters more than getting it fast — reasoning, careful writing, classroom dialogue. Google is best when the work spans formats: a video, a chart, a recording, all together.

What one student can do with all three.

The most useful way to picture this is at the level of a single student running a venture. Each of the three providers handles a different kind of work in her week: research, outreach, and analysis. None of them substitutes for her judgment. All of them compress the labor between an idea and a first version. What used to take a four-person team now takes one motivated undergrad and three accounts.

Where each provider has landed.

This is not theoretical. Wharton put ChatGPT in every MBA student's hands in 2024. Oxford opened campus-wide access in September 2025. Northeastern signed campus-wide with Anthropic across thirteen global campuses. Rice deployed Gemini institution-wide. The pattern across the field: each elite peer picked at least one of the three. Babson currently has none.

Scene 6 · The math

Roughly one endowed chair, per year, per provider.

For an institution Babson's size — roughly 5,000 active users — each of the three providers works out to about $0.9–$1.2 million per year at list pricing. That's the same order of magnitude as a single endowed faculty chair, recurring rather than one-time, and reaching every active member of campus rather than one faculty line. The scale floor: California State University's deal with OpenAI was $17 million over 18 months for 500,000+ users.

None of this lands on empty ground.
